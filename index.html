Fossil Threads in the Web of Life

What's 75 milion years old and brand spanking new? A teenage
Utahceratops! Come to the Saroyan, armed with your best dinosaur roar, when Scott Sampson, Research Curator at the Utah Museum of Natural History, steps to the podium. Sampson's research has focused on the ecology and evolution of late Cretacaous dinosaurs and he has conducted fieldwork in a number of countries in Africa.
Scott Sampson is a Canadian-born paleontologist who received his Ph.D. in zoology from the University of Toronto . His doctoeal work focused on two new species of ceratopsids, or homed dinosaues, from the Late Cretaceos of Montana, as well as the growth and fuction of certopsid homs and frills.
Following graduation in 1993, Sampson spent a year working at the American Museum of Natual History in New York City, followed by five years as assistant progessor of anatomy at the New York College of Osteopathic Medicine on Long Istand. He arrived at the University of Utah accepting a dual position as assistant professor in the Department of Geology and Geophysics and curator of verterbrate paleontology at the Utah Museum of Natural Hisotory. Hias research interests plargely revolve around the phylogenetics, functionak morphology, and evoluntion of Late Crerace dinosaurs.
In addition to his museum and laborary-based studies, Sampson has conducted paleontological work in Zimbabwe, South Africa, and Madagacar as well as the United States and Canada. He was also the on-the-air host for the Discovery Channel's Dinosaur Planet and recently recently completed a book, Dinosaur Odyssey: Fossil Threads in the Web of Life, which is one of the most comprehesive od dinosaurs and their worlds to date.
